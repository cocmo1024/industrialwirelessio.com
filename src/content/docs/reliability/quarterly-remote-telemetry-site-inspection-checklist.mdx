# Quarterly Remote Telemetry Site Inspection Checklist

Remote telemetry systems do not usually fail in one dramatic step. They drift. A battery loses reserve. A cable gland loosens. Antenna signal quality gets worse after a nearby change. A cabinet begins collecting condensation. A digital input still changes, but its alarm path no longer reaches the right person. A dashboard looks live because one value updates while the important event stream is silently stale.

A quarterly inspection is a controlled way to catch that drift. It should be more than a photo visit and less than full recommissioning. The goal is to prove that the site is still physically survivable, electrically healthy, communicative, operationally useful, and supportable.

## Quick answer

A quarterly remote telemetry inspection should verify cabinet condition, power reserve, antenna and backhaul quality, sensor and I/O behavior, alarm delivery, stale-data handling, local buffering, documentation accuracy, and recovery readiness. The technician should leave with evidence, not just a statement that the site looked normal.

## Why quarterly inspection matters

Unattended sites create a specific reliability problem: the system may be important only during abnormal conditions, but those are exactly the conditions that reveal neglected installation details.

Examples:

- A flood-control site looks fine during dry weather but fails when the cabinet takes moisture.
- A remote tank site reports normally until a weak battery causes a reset during cold starts.
- A cellular router stays online but signal quality degrades enough to cause intermittent SCADA loss.
- A pump station alarm contact changes locally but the upstream alarm rule has been disabled or rerouted.
- A site has a working gateway but nobody can find the latest configuration backup when replacement is needed.

Quarterly inspection is not bureaucracy. It is a way to reduce blind spots before the next event.

## Inspection scope

Use a consistent scope so teams can compare sites across time:

| Area | Inspection question | Evidence to keep |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cabinet | Is the enclosure still sealed, labeled, dry, and serviceable? | Photos, door seal check, cable entry condition |
| Power | Is supply voltage stable and backup reserve credible? | Voltage readings, charger status, battery age |
| Backhaul | Is cellular, radio, fiber, or satellite quality acceptable? | Signal-quality readings and reconnect test |
| Field signals | Do critical inputs, meters, and sensors still match reality? | Forced-state or comparison checks |
| Alarms | Do assert, clear, latch, and notification paths still work? | Alarm test records with timestamps |
| Data quality | Are values fresh, scaled correctly, and labeled correctly upstream? | Screenshot or exported point verification |
| Recovery | Can the site be restored after outage or device replacement? | Backup, config version, credential path |
| Handoff | Can operations and support understand the site later? | As-built note updates and exceptions list |

## Cabinet and environment checks

Start with the physical cabinet because physical problems create strange telemetry symptoms later.

Check:

- enclosure damage, corrosion, sun exposure, mounting stability, and door alignment;
- gasket condition, water marks, insect intrusion, dust, or condensation;
- cable glands, conduit entries, drain or vent condition, and unused openings;
- labels on gateway, RTU, power supply, fuses, I/O, terminal blocks, and field wiring;
- service clearance around replaceable components;
- surge protection status indicators where present;
- grounding and bonding connections that are visible without disturbing the installation.

Take cabinet photos with labels readable. A blurry wide photo is weak evidence. Future support needs to know what is installed, how it is powered, and whether the cabinet condition changed.

## Power and backup inspection

Power inspection should capture current health and reserve risk.

Record:

- incoming supply voltage under normal load;
- DC power supply output and fuse status;
- battery voltage, age, charger state, and any visible swelling or corrosion;
- solar controller status if present;
- UPS or battery backup alarm state;
- load changes since the last inspection;
- whether recent outages caused resets or stale data.

Do not treat "powered on" as enough. Remote telemetry sites often fail during transition: utility loss, generator transfer, cold start, weak battery, or unstable solar recovery.

## Antenna and backhaul checks

For wireless sites, inspect both the physical antenna path and the network readings.

Capture:

- antenna mount condition, orientation, cable route, connector weatherproofing, and strain relief;
- cable length or any added adapter that was not in the original design;
- cellular RSRP, RSRQ, SINR, or vendor-equivalent signal values;
- radio RSSI/noise margin or satellite terminal status where applicable;
- reconnect time after a controlled modem/router restart if allowed;
- evidence of blocked line of sight, new metal structures, vegetation, or changed site conditions.

Signal strength is not enough. Poor signal quality can leave the router "online" while protocol traffic behaves badly.

## Sensor, I/O, and point checks

A quarterly visit should verify the points that operators rely on during abnormal conditions. Do not test every low-value point if the site has many. Test the points that drive alarms, dispatch, compliance, safety awareness, or production decisions.

For each critical point:

- compare analog values with a local gauge, instrument, meter, or expected condition;
- force or simulate a digital input when operationally safe;
- confirm scaling, engineering units, and decimal placement upstream;
- verify totalizer or pulse counts against expected change;
- confirm that disabled, bypassed, or failed sensors are marked clearly;
- note any field condition that makes the reading misleading.

The inspection should catch the difference between "the gateway is connected" and "the data still means what operations thinks it means."

## Alarm and stale-data testing

Alarm testing is where many inspections are too shallow.

Test:

- alarm assert and clear behavior;
- latching behavior where required;
- notification routing to the right destination;
- stale-data or heartbeat alarm behavior;
- local buffering during a short controlled link outage where safe;
- alarm timestamps after reconnect;
- whether repeated nuisance alarms have been suppressed without review.

If operations relies on the site during emergencies, the alarm path must be tested during normal inspection windows.

## Documentation and recovery evidence

Every inspection should reduce future recovery time. Update:

- cabinet photos;
- network path notes;
- SIM, APN, VPN, carrier, or private network notes where appropriate;
- gateway or RTU model and firmware;
- I/O map changes;
- configuration backup location;
- password or credential ownership path, without putting secrets in the inspection report;
- unresolved exceptions and assigned owner.

Documentation is part of reliability. A site that works but cannot be restored quickly after hardware replacement is not fully supportable.

## Pass, watch, fail

Use a simple status model:

| Status | Meaning | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pass | Site meets inspection criteria and evidence is complete | Keep normal cadence |
| Watch | Site works but has visible drift or incomplete evidence | Assign correction before next quarter |
| Fail | Site has unreliable alarm, data, power, communication, or recovery behavior | Open corrective work immediately |

This prevents vague inspection notes from hiding real risk.
