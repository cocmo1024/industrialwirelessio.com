# Report by Exception and Store-and-Forward

Remote telemetry systems often work better when they transmit less, not more. In field deployments with constrained power, unstable backhaul, and sparse maintenance windows, aggressive polling can create noise and fragile behavior. Report-by-exception and store-and-forward patterns exist because remote sites need to stay useful when communications are imperfect.

## Which layer this decision belongs to

This is less about a named protocol and more about telemetry behavior. The key question is how the site should act when:

- values change materially;
- alarms or thresholds are crossed;
- links are temporarily unavailable;
- operators need confidence that important events were not lost.

Those decisions shape device choice, buffering needs, and alarm strategy.

## Typical fit

These patterns are a good fit when:

- the site mainly needs alarm visibility and trend snapshots;
- the power budget rewards low-traffic communications;
- backhaul quality is variable;
- operators care more about seeing meaningful changes than constant chatter.

This is why remote utilities, tanks, and scattered assets often benefit from exception-driven behavior.

## Common field mistake

A frequent mistake is designing remote telemetry as if the link were a stable on-premise network. Constant polling can waste power, increase cost, and still fail to preserve the events that matter most when coverage drops.

The better question is usually: what must arrive immediately, what can be buffered, and what can wait.

## Hardware and reliability consequences

These behavior choices affect:

- whether the device class needs reliable local buffering;
- how alarm thresholds are configured and tested;
- battery and solar sizing assumptions;
- how much field troubleshooting is required after communication interruptions.

They are operational decisions, not just protocol preferences.
