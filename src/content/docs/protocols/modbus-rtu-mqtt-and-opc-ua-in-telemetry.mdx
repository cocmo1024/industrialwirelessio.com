# Modbus RTU, MQTT, and OPC UA in Telemetry

Modbus RTU, MQTT, and OPC UA are often discussed as if they are competing answers to one question. In real remote telemetry systems, they usually belong at different layers. Modbus RTU is often closest to legacy field devices. MQTT often fits event-oriented movement across constrained or cloud-connected paths. OPC UA often fits richer industrial interoperability when systems need structured context and stronger integration semantics.

The risk is choosing a protocol by popularity instead of by layer. A remote site can use Modbus RTU locally, convert or buffer at a gateway, publish MQTT upstream, and still expose structured data elsewhere through OPC UA. The architecture matters more than the label.

## Quick answer

Use Modbus RTU near field devices when legacy equipment, meters, drives, or simple controllers already speak it. Use MQTT when the telemetry path benefits from lightweight publish-style movement, report-by-exception, and broker-based distribution. Use OPC UA when the upstream environment needs richer context, industrial interoperability, and structured information models. Do not force one protocol to solve every layer.

## Layer model

| Layer | Common protocol fit | What the layer should own |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Field device layer | Modbus RTU, vendor serial, discrete/analog I/O | Reading actual site signals and device registers |
| Local cabinet or gateway layer | Modbus TCP, gateway mapping, local logic, buffering | Polling, translation, deadbands, timestamps, store-and-forward |
| Transport and event layer | MQTT, HTTPS APIs, brokered telemetry | Efficient upstream movement and event distribution |
| Supervisory or integration layer | OPC UA, SCADA protocols, historian connectors | Context, naming, system interoperability, operator visibility |

Many failed telemetry projects happen because the team skips the gateway layer and expects the upstream platform to clean up field ambiguity later.

## Modbus RTU strengths and limits

Modbus RTU is durable because it is simple and common. It is useful for:

- meters;
- analyzers;
- drives;
- packaged equipment;
- legacy controllers;
- and small remote devices with stable register maps.

Its weakness is context. A register value does not explain whether the signal is stale, noisy, scaled correctly, or meaningful under the current site state. That context usually has to be added by the RTU, gateway, SCADA, or historian layer.

## MQTT strengths and limits

MQTT is attractive in telemetry because it can support efficient publish-style data movement. It fits when:

- the site should report by exception;
- bandwidth or data-plan discipline matters;
- several consumers need the same telemetry stream;
- cloud ingestion or broker-based routing is part of the design;
- and the team can define topic structure, payload contracts, and retained-message behavior.

MQTT becomes weak when teams treat it as magic structure. A broker does not fix bad signal naming, weak event logic, poor timestamps, or unclear ownership of units and state.

## OPC UA strengths and limits

OPC UA can be useful when the system needs richer industrial context, not just lightweight transport. It often fits:

- plant-level integration;
- supervisory systems;
- structured equipment models;
- vendor-neutral interoperability;
- and environments where OT systems already expect OPC UA.

For small remote telemetry sites, OPC UA may be heavier than necessary unless there is a clear consumer that benefits from it.

## Protocol decision checklist

Before choosing the stack, answer:

1. Which devices must be read locally?
2. Where will scaling, units, naming, and bad-quality handling be applied?
3. What happens during network loss?
4. Which system is the first trustworthy consumer of the data?
5. Does the upstream platform need events, current state, history, or structured equipment context?
6. Who will troubleshoot protocol failures at 2 a.m.?
